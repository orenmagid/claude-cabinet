The Problem

The Rogers Connection

In a COUN 6000 seminar, a graduate student argued that Carl Rogers's three facilitative conditions — congruence, unconditional positive regard, and empathy — apply to writing as much as therapy. Academic prose that hides behind passive constructions and unnecessary nominalizations isn't just hard to read. It's incongruent — the writer's actual meaning is buried under performance. Jargon used to signal membership rather than convey meaning violates unconditional positive regard: it imposes comprehension as a condition of worth.

Four Source Authorities

Helen Sword — Stylish Academic Writing (2012). William Zinsser — On Writing Well (2006). Steven Pinker — The Sense of Style (2014). Joseph Williams & Joseph Bizup — Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace (2014). Four different approaches to the same conviction: clear writing is not a compromise with rigor. It's a sign of rigor.

Reading Four Books

What Extraction Means

Not summaries. Not "key takeaways." Actionable rewriting operations — specific things you do to a sentence. "When the subject of a sentence is an abstract nominalization, rewrite with a human character as the subject and the buried action as the verb." That's a principle. "Zinsser says to cut clutter" is not.

Three Skills, One Framework

The Settings System

Five independent knobs, each with a vocabulary of values:

voice: preserve | blend | translate
jargon: preserve | selective | replace
density: preserve | tighten | compress
structure: preserve | refine | restructure
fidelity: strict | charitable | liberal
 
Presets:
copyedit — lightest touch
clarify — readable but recognizable
rewrite — full transformation
reimagine — rebuild from meaning

Jargon Taxonomy

Structural terms (like "formulation" in psychology) must be earned — defined on first use, then used precisely. Field shorthand (like "evidence-based") gets replaced with plain language and a footnote preserving the original. Decorative jargon (like "the process of making sense of") gets cut. Three categories, three different treatments.

Original

However, the ability to master this skill is confounded by a range of factors. These include the myriad and often complex reasons for which clients seek help, implicit agendas driving the request for help of which the practitioner may not be aware, and the extent to which individuals are able to identify, describe, and address their needs within a psychological framework.

Rewritten

Formulation — building a psychological explanation of a client's problems to guide treatment — is one of the most important skills a psychologist can develop. Over a career, much of a practitioner's time goes to exactly this: making sense of the psychological puzzles clients bring. But formulation is hard to master, for reasons on both sides of the room. Clients seek help for complicated, overlapping reasons. They may carry hidden agendas they aren't aware of themselves. They differ in how well they can name what's wrong and what they need.

S1: "is deemed to be" — passive nominalization hiding the claim. "the process of making sense" — nominalization burying the action. S3: passive voice ("is deemed," "will be spent"). S9: "over the course of our careers" → "over a career." Structural jargon "formulation" earned with inline definition on first use.
S1: "the ability to master" / "the extent to which individuals are able to" — nominalizations replaced with characters doing things. S7: one 87-word sentence → five shorter sentences, each one thought. S9: "a range of factors," "play a critical role in decision making" — clutter cut.

What the Footnotes Mean

Every change is annotated with the principle code that motivated it. S1 is the character/action principle (make a human the subject, make their action the verb). S7 is sentence length. S9 is clutter.
