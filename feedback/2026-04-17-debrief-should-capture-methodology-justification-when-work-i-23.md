## Debrief should capture methodology justification when work is painstaking or novel — and some of it wants to be narrateable outside the room

**Friction:** Worked through the Phase 3 ST pilot synthesis today — a
painstaking methodology with calibration harness, disposition register,
two-pass reviewer design, plant-and-verify choreography. Near the end,
I realized the method record needed more than "what we decided" — it
needed the vulnerable edges (where we extrapolated, what evidence we
had, where a skeptic could press, what would change our approach). I
wrote that layer only because the user explicitly asked for
justification that can be critiqued. Under standard debrief, decisions
and outcomes would likely have been preserved but the reasoning chain
and self-critique layer would have been lost — and so would the story.

**Suggestion:** Not a required phase for every session. Most sessions
don't warrant this, and forcing it would be bureaucratic. But when a
session produces new process, decides load-bearing methodology, or
establishes a pattern that future sessions will consume, debrief could
optionally prompt for a two-layer capture:

1. **Internal justification-and-vulnerabilities layer** — for insiders,
   including future-self. For each decision: the claim, the evidence
   actually in hand, the skeptic's critique, the counter, and what
   would change the approach. This is the inspect-and-challenge layer;
   it keeps reasoning chains alive instead of ossifying into "how we've
   always done it."

2. **Narrateable layer — for external audiences that might care.** Not
   every detail; just enough for a good story. "We extracted 1,087
   principles across 10 sources. Consolidated them via a gated
   protocol with independent inter-rater calibration and plant-and-
   verify defect seeding. The statistical-translation pass alone cites
   138 entries across 7 sources and produces 28 principles + 13
   procedures with full provenance." Investors might care. Customers
   might care. Collaborators might care. The founder, moving fast and
   barely hanging on across a dozen workstreams, might care most of
   all — because without a narrateable version, even the person who
   lived it loses the thread.

**Detection heuristics:** new skill created, pib-db projects named
"Phase N...", audit artifacts written to \, \, or
\, multi-subagent choreography, gate scripts with pass/
fail thresholds, disposition registers. When signals fire, debrief
asks: *"This session looks like methodology work. Want to capture an
internal critique-edges record? Want to draft an external narrative
summary?"* Either, both, or neither — user's call.

**The test for "core":** not every session, but when the work is core
to the product's differentiation or legitimacy — the thing the
founder would want on a pitch deck or in an about page, the thing a
customer would ask "how do I know this works?" about — methodology
deserves capture in a form that can be shared, not just internalized.

The cost of not having this: the methodology ossifies, the
founder loses the thread to their own work, and there's no good
answer to "walk me through how you built this" when someone who
matters asks.

**Session context:** Phase 3 ST pilot in the medico-legal framework
extension — synthesized 138 raw entries into 28 principles + 13
procedures, ran a two-pass calibration harness (lenient then
adversarial), found the adversarial prompt surfaces real issues the
lenient one misses. Produced a ~6,000-word method record with 10
justification sections and five named "uncomfortable truths" — all
only after explicit user prompting. The founder has multiple
concurrent workstreams (auth, observability, medico-legal, UX v2,
Ko-fi integration) and noted being "barely hanging on" — the
methodology's own narrateability is a survival tool, not a luxury.
